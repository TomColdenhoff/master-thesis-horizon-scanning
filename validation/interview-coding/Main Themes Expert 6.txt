Theme 1: The concept is immediately recognized as relevant by a public sector legal expert

Expert 5 understood what the tool does and confirmed its relevance within the first few minutes, without needing to see the interface first. His framing was direct: as head of legal affairs at multiple large municipalities, he was continuously asked by management whether an incoming law was coming soon and whether the organization could start preparing. That question ("can we get ahead of this?") is exactly the problem the tool addresses. He was also quick to point out that courts already factor in anticipated regulatory change when making decisions, which means anticipating legislation is not just useful but already part of legal practice at every level. This is a different kind of validation than the other participants offered. Expert 5 does not validate the tool as a piece of software but as a solution to a problem he personally dealt with throughout his entire career.

Theme 2: The tool's value is dual: preparation and influence, not just monitoring

Expert 5 introduced a distinction that no other participant made as clearly. When a signal is detected, an organization can do two things: prepare internally, and try to influence the outcome. He gave both sides equal weight. On the preparation side, a municipality needs to arrange knowledge, staff, capacity, and budget before a new legal obligation takes effect, and that process takes time. On the influence side, early detection gives organizations the window to lobby, send representatives to parliament, or coordinate through platforms like the Brainport Office in Brussels. He was explicit that the ability to influence is the more valuable of the two uses, and that it only works if the signal is detected early enough. This dual framing substantially broadens the validated use case beyond compliance and into active political participation, which is a new contribution relative to the other interviews.

Theme 3: Tracking signal development over time is the most important missing feature

Expert 5 immediately pointed out that a single signal is just the beginning. Before a law is enacted, there are follow-up letters, committee hearings, parliamentary debates, and many other documents. What users actually need is to follow that chain from early signal to enacted law, actively and continuously. He asked whether the system could do this. This is the clearest description of a gap across all five interviews. Where other participants focused on the design and usability of individual signal cards, Expert 5 looked at the broader workflow and identified that a single-document snapshot is not enough. Tracking how a signal develops over time is what transforms the tool from an alert system into a genuine horizon scanning instrument.

Theme 4: The tool should replace subjective expert judgment with objective analysis

The goat farming example is the most concrete demonstration of the tool's value in any of the five interviews. Expert 5 described a real situation in his own village where a ministerial letter about required distances from goat farms was directly relevant to a local housing plan. He read the letter himself and formed an estimate (500 meters, not 1000 meters), but acknowledged it was based on a gut feeling. He then said unprompted that it would be much more useful to have that estimate produced by an objective analysis of all the relevant documents. This is a direct statement that the tool's core output, a structured, source-grounded signal assessment, is exactly what experienced practitioners currently do by hand. No other participant described the tool as a replacement for their own expert judgment; Expert 5 did.

Theme 5: The validated user base is wider than legal professionals and includes municipalities, provinces, waterboards, and large companies

Expert 5 confirmed at the end of the interview that large companies are in the same position as municipalities: they need to monitor, prepare for, and influence regulation. He also pointed to provinces, waterboards, and lobby organizations as natural users. Combined with his description of the Brainport network in Brussels and the municipal office in The Hague, this shows a broad ecosystem of organizations that already dedicate resources to exactly the problem the tool addresses. This matches the finding from the semiconductor industry interview about cross-departmental relevance, and together the two interviews confirm that the tool addresses a structural need across the public and private sectors, not just a niche use case in legal research.

Summary:

Expert 5 provides the most practice-grounded validation of any participant in this study. He confirms the concept from decades of experience as a municipal legal professional and head of legal affairs, and he validates it not as a technology product but as an answer to a recurring operational need. His most important contributions are: the need to anticipate regulation is already embedded in legal and judicial practice; early signal detection enables both internal preparation and external lobbying, with lobbying being the higher-value use case; the tool needs to track signal development over time, not just detect initial signals; and objective analysis should replace the subjective expert judgment that practitioners currently rely on. Across all five interviews, four findings are consistent: card compactness and scannability, signal reliability context, post-review workflow, and the strategic use case for long-term planning. Expert 5 adds one finding that is unique to this interview: continuous longitudinal tracking of signals is the feature that would make the tool genuinely useful in practice, rather than just interesting as a proof of concept.
